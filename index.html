Correlations Discovered Between Health Risks and Poverty, Age, and Income


As we can observe from the data that was pulled from the U.S. Census Bureau and the Behavioral Risk Factor Surveillance System in 2014, there are clear signs of a positive correlation between poverty and various health risks. We can see that with each state, as the poverty as a percentage of the population increased, so did the percentage of people who lack healthcare, the percentage of the population that smokes, and the obesity rate.

With age as the dependent variable, the various health risks present correlations, although not as clear as with poverty. As age increases, we can see a slight positive correlation with obesity, but a large chunk of the data seems to be centered with a high number of obesity rates centering around the 26-36% range of the population. The percentage of the population that smokes sees a more positive correlation with age, but generally there are less Americans that smoke than are those who are obese, with the majority range lying between 14-26% of the population. Finally, with a lack of healthcare, there is a clear negative correlation with age, as the older Americans are, the less likely they are to lack healthcare. The percentage of the population that lacks healthcare in all states fluctuates primarily in the 7-18% range, with the higher median age yielding lower numbers in said range.

The final dependent variable we can observe is median household income. It's clear that as a state becomes richer, the health risks presented become less prominent. The most obese state, Mississipi, capped at a median household income of around $40,000 and obesity rates of 35.5%, while the second least obese, Washington, D.C., saw a median household income of around $72,000 and obesity rates of 21.7%. There's a clear trend in a similar fashion between household income and the percentage of population that smokes, as well, albeit the percentage of smokers in the United States is significantly lower than the percentage of obese. Finally, there is also a clear negative correlation between median household income and lack of healthcare. The majority of states lie between the 7-19% range of populations that lack healthcare, with the lowest median incomes yielding less healthcare coverage.

In conclusion, there is a clear positive correlation between poverty and health risks, and aversely, negative correlations between higher median household income and the aforementioned risks. With age, the picture is not so clear, but there is a slight trend towards more healthcare coverage and more smokers as age increases.
